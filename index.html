Fighting for Freedom ... to the death, because it's your life they took

Gratitude for the everyday, Paul Robinson shares joys and hardships since his release

Written by Joli Holmes and Jovi Dai

This story documents Paul Robinson's life after his release from prison, where he served 52 years. Paul maintains his innocence and is working with the New England Innocence Project (NEIP) to overturn his conviction in his fight for justice. We spoke to Paul in January 2023, approximately eight months after release.

For those of us on the outside, it’s easy to imagine that release day, the day an incarcerated person is released from prison, is filled with positive emotions — joy, optimism, gratitude, and happiness. And while most previously incarcerated people feel these or similar emotions, those aren’t the only ones. For any incarcerated person, re-entry is an inflection point that brings about happiness but isn’t devoid of challenges. Some of the challenges of re-entry have been particularly acute for Paul, a now 76-year-old man who entered the criminal justice system in the 1970s for a crime he maintains he did not didn’t commit.

Paul was committed prior to the invention of the Internet, smartphones, and even cell phones. He is used to paying for items with cash. People played music on audio cassettes and Walkmans. The neighborhood he grew up in, Roslindale, looks and feels completely different than it did 52 years ago.

Paul holds an art piece that says 'Fighting for Freedom to the death, because it's your life they took.'
